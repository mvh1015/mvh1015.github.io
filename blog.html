DigJumpDrill: An Informal PostMortem

9/22/2017


Here I am, typing this up at 6:00 AM after a long night of finishing our Final Project, DigJumpDrill, an endless runner. Ten weeks, my partner and I have been working on DigJumpDrill, with some highs, but mostly lows. I figured it would just start off as another school project where you group up with a partner, start brainstorming ideas, and start building a prototype. This project started a little different though.


The first week began, and my partner already had a plan and a game design document. This was a bit unexpected, but I figured that it could be fun following blindly into this. After a few suggestions, we ended up with an endless runner for an idea. My partner wanted it to be 3D, because he wanted to focus on learning 3D character creation. I was hoping for 3D, because I wanted to make a well-structured and stable game, since we only had ten weeks to complete the assignment. We settled on 2.5D and started the prototype.


I began to think about what this game needed, and where we were going with it. I decided that the game needed to be fair to the player, and needed to scale fairly in difficulty. I felt like just speeding up the game and throwing random obstacles would just be cheating. Instead, I came up with a whole plan for procedurally generating the endless levels. I drew out diagrams with tiers and hierarchies with names such as "sectors", "sections", and "segments".


Eventually I got to the point where I programmed it. It took a couple of weeks to get it working the way I wanted. I was able to customize what obstacles came and increase the difficulty as the game went by. It was structured in such a way that I could create dynamic difficulty based on how well the player was doing. I even created a simple instruction editor sheet, which a monkey could follow, if someone wanted to create their own sectors or segments.


Finally, it was all done, and it was time to test to playtest it. At that point I realized, nobody could even see behind all of the code I did. It was just as random to them, as a simple random number generator. Players didn't care about the difficulty. Most got bored after a minute, before half my my system even kicked in. This is when I found out how boring our game actually was. All you could do was dig, jump, and drill. Three things.


I didn't want a boring game. How could i get people to want to play it? "It needs something more." This is the quote that I kept repeating for the final four weeks. My partner felt the same way, but he had plenty of detailing and work to do to all of the game art assets, so I was basically on my own when it came to making the mechanics and progression better for the players.


I playtested the other groups' games and was trying to find out what made each one fun. I noticed people having the most fun with trying to get highscores. My game didn't have a highscore yet, so that's exactly what I started working on. I realized that after getting a high score in my game, it was hard to motivate yourself to play again and beat that highscore. The game needed some kind of progression system. Combining a progression system and highscores meant that I would need to create a player profile system. That was exactly what I did next.


I created a system where you could log in and out of a player name. Each player name could keep and own their own progress. What progress could we give them though. My first idea was to create a shop. Allow them to collect coins in game, and use these coins to upgrade their power ups, allowing them to last longer. After doing this, it still felt like it needed more. The power up upgrades did help the player last longer, allowing them to beat their previous highscores more, but they still weren't motivated to do so.


At this point, my partner is just creating more art each day, and is getting worried that I am going to keep adding more and more, which may conflict with his plans. I knew he was worried about this, so I did as much as I could to create fun, without creating any extra design projects for him to work on. I tried adding more items to the shop, like extra lives, but it was barely helping.


It was getting very close to the deadline, so I realized that adding more and more features at this point wasn't a good idea. I focused on the small details, particle effects, sounds, and transitions. I started to add some humor to the game, hoping this would distract people from the other boring parts. The game started to feel a lot better, especially with the new super powerup, with a heavy metal riff when you collected a special powerup.


Late last night, I still wasn't very happy with the game. Even though I felt like we were going to get a good grade on it, it still needed more. It bugged me. There was no reward for playing the game and nothing to achieve. Even though I knew I'd be up all night, I decided to solve this problem in one of the simplest ways possible, by adding achievements. When the player gets these achievements, such as playing ten games, or collecting 1000 coins in a single game, a score multiplier is added. The more achievements they get, the higher score they get. This means that people who play more, will have a huge advantage at getting to the highscore leaderboard. It might be cheap and dirty, but it made me feel a lot better about the game.


After this ten week project, there were plenty of small technical things I learned, such as data persistence and Unity's animator, but I felt like the biggest problem I had to tackle was creating a fun environment for the player. At first, I was thinking too logically, but the problem with that is, people don't play games to be logical. Most players just like pretty things that make them feel good, like explosions and achievements. One thing I wish my game did was challenge the player. Currently, the game is just a grind. I did make the game a little bit more fast paced, but it wasn't enough, and if I made it any faster, it would just become unfair. My biggest issue that I need to work on when it comes to making games, is balancing reward and challenge.
